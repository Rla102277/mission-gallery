Most of us spend our lives just outside the frame.

We scroll past the image, save it for later, and move on. The daydream is the beautiful moment we never quite inhabit.

I started chasing the places that couldn’t be scrolled past.

The Infinite Arch is built on a belief that the most honest photographs aren’t taken — they’re waited for. They come to the photographer who arrives early, stays late, and returns when the light changes. Who understands that the arch in the rock and the arch of a life are describing the same passage: a threshold between what was and what’s possible.

I shoot with Fujifilm’s GFX medium format system because the format demands presence. You can’t spray and pray at 102 megapixels. You choose your moment, and you mean it.

This is photography as contemplation. Landscape as mirror. Light as metaphor.

Welcome to the other side of the daydream.

“You don’t make a photograph just with a camera. You bring to the act of photography all the pictures you have seen, the books you have read, the music you have heard, the people you have loved.”

— Ansel Adams

“To see the world, things dangerous to come to. To see behind walls, draw closer, to find each other and to feel. That is the purpose of life.”

“Number 25 is my best ever, the quintessence of life, I think..”

— Sean O’Connell  ·  The Secret Life of Walter Mitty

Number 25 isn’t technically perfect or compositionally superior. It is the quintessence of life. The difference matters to me. We are talking about a photograph that captures something true — not flawless, but undeniably alive. That’s the standard I’m chasing. Not the image that impresses, but the one that’s irreplaceable. The one I had to be cold for, had to wait for, had to raise the camera toward the impossible light and believe something was there before I could see it. This is what I came for. This is the quintessence of life.
